EntropicSurge: Chaotic Latents are Principled Image-to-Video Attackers

Anonymous Author(s)
Code(upcoming)

Abstract

Text-guided image-to-video (TI2V) generation with latent diffusion models enables the synthesis of temporally coherent videos from static images and text prompts, offering creative potential but also posing risks such as the unauthorized transformation of personal or copyrighted images into misleading or harmful content. Existing defenses are inadequate: image-based adversarial methods fail against TI2V's temporal consistency, while video-specific approaches often rely on heuristic frame-level perturbations that lack theoretical grounding, scalability, and robustness across architectures.

In this paper, we propose EntropicSurge, a lightweight, transferable, and theoretically principled distribution-level attack grounded in information theory. Rather than perturbing individual video frames, EntropicSurge increases the entropy of the latent distribution generated by the variational autoencoder (VAE), injecting variance during the initialization phase of TI2V synthesis. This heightened entropy induces substantial divergence in the sampling process, thereby degrading both spatial fidelity and temporal coherence in the resulting video.

Under the assumption of a Gaussian prior, we show that entropy maximization aligns with latent variance augmentation, yielding a simple yet powerful objective for attack optimization. By operating at the distribution level of the shared VAE encoder, EntropicSurge is inherently agnostic to video length and exhibits strong generalization across TI2V model architectures.

Comprehensive experiments demonstrate that EntropicSurge surpasses existing defense strategies in effectiveness, computational efficiency, and cross-model transferability.
